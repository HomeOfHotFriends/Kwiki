Introduction
Introduction 
My name is Karl Leisky. 
My life has been characterised by shit luck. Mental health issues, random acts of violence, endings before I could even start. As a result of these things, I ended up on ACC, New Zealand’s universal health insurance. First three years I simply rotted. Then I remembered my talent. The last two years have been the happiest of my life.
As a human, I carry only one truth. I can create. I have practised so much I don’t even have to try. I sit down on my throne and shit out creations. 
Recently, I finished an album called Frog In A Pot.

I hate releasing my work, I create simply because if I don’t I want to die. The only other times my brain is at peace is when I’m talking to friends or going for walks. I think those things are part of creation. But I release so the project is done, and I can move on to the next. Frog In A Pot was released on soundcloud to little fanfare.
Three days later, I received a pile of pages in the mail.  Printed on a strange fibrous stock, the pages weighed heavy in my hands. Attached to the front was a note written in a looping hand.


Karl,

We must apologise. 
Sorry.
You do not know us, and we doubt we will ever meet. Yet we are the cause of all your suffering. An “I” of ours sent his consciousness back in time. Our “I” found you, and task in its last moments left you with an impossible task. Born a we into a system that tries to make the “I”, we made you an outsider. You have been carrying two “I”s inside you your entire life. Who knows how many souls you carry inside you now, all these years later. 


We have attached the manuscript for a book a dear friend wrote. The Centre for Earthling Neutrality has destroyed all other copies. They will never allow it to be published in my world, but maybe it could be in yours. 

Please take on the role of the editor. You’re the only one who can.


Platonically yours,
A Galactic “We”


P.S.
We wanted to congratulate you on your release of Frog In A Pot. It is everything we dreamed of.

First Home
First Home
Outside, the world hummed an ordinary, run-down tune. Markets took up the majority of the streets Fishermangs brought in the day’s catch; at a vintage clothes stall, a sacred item waited. 


[FOR SALE: Red Hat]


A strange off-world visitor tried it on and said, “Fits.”  There was laughter. 
Of course it did. 
The plink-plinking joy never reached my ears.


BPM: 125
Inside, The Flotsam was fog and late-stage existentialism over salted fish. 


Behind the bar: me and the dirty cups left unwashed from other workers’ shifts.


He just appeared as if he’d already been here a while. Standing by an old poster advertising a Spounge-Singers of Xybin tour.


I remember the way he slouched; not an affect but part of himb, as if his skeleton carried a memory of being much shorter. [Himb? Cute but reads meme-speak —Ed.]


Outside, drones swarmed as market sellers hid their contraband.


Inside, the room thinned to a hush—except for the jukebox, still looping CEN-approved Shrielian jazz. A torn speaker cone distorted the sound with the buzz of orange juice on a flu-stricken tongue.


A red hat sat backwards on a long noodle head. The hat’s button grazed the beams; green flares as it lifts and falls. 


fwop, fwop, fwop, fwop.
Four beams between the poster and the bartop. 


Not only was he a good two feet taller than any Teauhan I’ve ever seen, but he wasn't wearing clothes from anywhere on the planet. 


He was wearing jeans and sneakers. Earth Style.


The visitor would be lucky if ol’ Eahrl in the corner didn’t try to start a fight. Old, yes, but could scrap with the best. 


Thankfully, the old fishermang was sleeping off a potent fish guts cocktail, the martini glass still steaming on the Teauhan oahk tabletop. Better clocked by Eahrl than a Neutrality Patrol Drone, I thought. You didn’t wanna be caught by one of those.


The noodle approached.


Wait a dogam minute—
My brain went ping ping ping.
—was that a Pavement tee? 


[Fucking love Pavement —Ed.]


It drew my attention more than any noodle head. I fizzed. 
I fretted that a drone would clock it.

I took my brain out of music to consider the other implications of his dress. This is Teauh. It’s Cahlmers. I was in my pinstripe blazer and jeans, I had my fur. 
This guy looked cold-blooded, with smooth, shiny, orange skin that looked like it’d crack from a single tap from the back of a spoon. 


And he was in a fucking T-shirt?


The noodle walked to the counter, opened his mouth, and ordered a double juice on the rocks.


I remember he said please.


“Yeah, what sorta juice you want?”
“Ya got Sprankle?”
“No one orders Sprankle ‘round here”


He leaned back on his heels, sneakers squeaking.


“You got it?”
I looked behind me.


[FOR SALE: Sprankle; Ingo Flavour]
[FOR SALE: Sprankle; Lumub Berry Flavour]
[OUT OF STOCK: Sprankle; Quambile Flavour]

“Of course. Ingo or lumub berry?” 
“Lumub, please”


He had an odd cadence to his speech and an alien musicality to his voice. The ‘d’ and ‘b’ in “double” resonated as if the blunt force of the consonant was striking a bell located in his chest. The ‘ck’ crackled as if a winter fire was roaring in his guts, and the ‘c’ in ‘juice’ crashed against his teeth like a wave hitting a pebbled beach. [This touched me. My parents once rushed a tiny me to hospital for heart palpitations; they said it sounded like bells —Ed.] 


He paid with two ridiculously crumpled notes. 


“How’s your day been?” he asked. 
“Oh, okay,” I said, “can’t complain.”

Out of habit, I turned around to wash cups. I looked back because sometimes you can’t avoid fate: “And yours?”


I only asked to hear the odd cadence of this visitor’s consonants again. 


That simple question would scour the rust off my life’s guitar, install a whammy bar;  a floating bridge.
 
“Oh, it’s been dope!”. His enthusiasm caught me off guard. 
“Yeah…?” I said. 
“Yeah! Life’s been good to be honest! I’m finally getting somewhere.” 
When he said this, he stood taller and his shoulders straightened. 


His hat was now completely flattened against the roof. 


He reeled me deeper into the conversation: 


“So what's your thing?”. 


“My… thing?” I said back. 
“Yeah mang”, he laughed. “What do you give a shit about?” 


No one had ever asked me that before, not like that. The question put a phaser on my power chord; it boosted the volume till it blew a wall out. 


I considered myself a connoisseur of Earth music. I went for the odd, the scrappy: bootleg concert flyers and lyrics scrawled on the receipt stub of a 2:00 a.m. Kebab. 


I figured that as long as I stayed quiet with it and stayed a consumer, I’d be left alone. Much like my favourite Earth musicians seemed to do with cannabis. [Can confirm —Ed.]


There was a streak of raw in the way those flaw-embracing Earthlings sang about cows and haircuts and friendships that made me want to bite something. Besides, I always liked an underdog.


A ten-page infodump clawed its way out of my mouth, almost unhinging my jaw; a desperate, breathless confession of every secret playlist and bootleg lyric I had ever loved. 


Even so, I was careful not to say ‘Earth’ explicitly.


I couldn’t stay still, hopping from chair to chair, from counter to sink and back again—a habit my parent called annoying. The noodle didn't bat an eye.


He introduced himbself. I had officially met Space Dad, the friend. 


I wondered who named himb.


“You ever been to Earth?” he asked, like crossing planets was just a short weekend trip. 


I stalled. I sweated. I couldn’t believe his gall—or was it just an attempt to rile me up? 


A Neutrality Drone outside the window turned inwards. I know he had seen it too. What was this space guy playing at? 
The drone flashed its sensor beam through the glass, just missing the word on his T-shirt. He must have seen my face: “I’m just passionate about footpaths”, he said with half a smirk; 


”You were saying about your governmentally-prescribed trip?”


The drone turned and drifted on.


“Never got the chance,” I admitted. “CEN’s blocklist isn’t something you can just erase.”


His hat raised, pushed by his eyebrows. 


I told himb my story, the same I’ll tell you now.


A rite of passage for every Teauhan is their post-school OE (On Earth) trip; a CEN indoctrination in all but name. Without it, you were nobody, your traditional tunic embarrassingly single-layered and bare. 

I never went.

Named Gharv Unkle, Gharv by my parent and Unkle by fate, I failed my simulation test. 


I used a direct humanisation of my name for my first—and let the randomiser pick my last. 


It spat out: Simon. 


I should have known better—but I didn’t listen to your parent’s Earth music.


In the corner, Eahrl woke up with a burp. I hoped Space Dad stayed turned to me, kept his sneakers tucked under himb.


“Full name?” asked the DMV clerk three days into my simulated Earth life. 
“Garfunkel Simon” I said. 
The clerk didn’t look up. “Alien Registry’s down the hall.” 


Space Dad laughed, and I smiled at the memory for the first time. “Fuck that band,” he said. 
“Not my thing,” I agreed.


My life seemed to be destined to stay in Teauh, more a collection of mud and salt water than a real planet.


Though my parent tried to be supportive, I could tell it was a source of embarrassment. I said I didn’t give a fuck.


No Earth patch meant no respectable CEN career, no entry into polite society.


CEN takes these failures seriously. Those who fail aren't just barred; they're flagged in the system. Any unauthorised contact with Earth after that... Well, it’s a risk, that’s all I’m saying.

The lucky got a fine. Everyone else got sent to Stream— Main’s prison moon.


A mate’s cousin, Khyle, went through Stream’s Hallway of Humanness. Turned into goo. 


There was a thud in the corner. Ol’ Eahrl stood up violently and ran outside to vom. “See you tomorrow Eahrl!” I shouted. A string of gags echoed into the night. “—is that… fuggin… Earth—URGH.”
“OUT!” I pointed at the door.

“What happened to Khyle?” Space Dad asked. 
“Kept in the cupboard next to the sourdough starter. Gets read the news every morning,” I replied, “Pfffts in reply.”


Space Dad’s questions were all oriented around the integration of the CEN into Teauh culture, though his garb hinted at a more vested interest.

When we had finished, he played his part and told me of his planet.  
It was then I learned of Home. A tiny (in planetary terms) orb with arms and legs sticking out. Wiggling away in the void of space.


He told me of the inhabitants. Plantlings, he called them: “What's incredible about Plantlings is that every single one is kind and cool. We might look playful on the outside, and that’s because we are!”


He told me about the parties. “Started as a big group of plantlings gathering under a rainbow geyser,” he said with a glow of cheekiness, “Then we discovered off-your-nut nuts. One minute, it's just a few bored plantlings with a nice view, next it's a whole dogam rager.”


Most of all, he told me about the music.


“It started 10 years ago. My mate Hat Mang called me up one evening and told me to get down to his workshop,” Space Dad said, pausing to glug approximately half the juice in one go. 


“His workshop was in a cute wee shed deep in Frogling Forest, by a bend in the River Tickle, which ran through the trees. 


When I arrived, Hat Mang was all a-fluster—mumbling about tones, walking back and forth. Two posts had been bored into the ground beside the shed, with a thick piece of wire cord running between them. 


When he finally noticed me, Hat Mang ran to the wire. ‘Check this out’, he said, and plucked the cord with a jittery, almost manic motion. 


‘BWOOOOOOM’ went the wire, and the surface of the river shimmered. ‘Yeah, that’s sound,’ I told himb. ‘We’ve known about sound for ages.’” 


Something about that last sentence stopped my bouncing. His voice was calm, I got  the jitters. I picked a seat and sat down, legs thrice woven. Sound, yes, but did that mean?


“‘Yeah, but now watch,’ Hat Mang said. 


He grabbed a round rock set by one of the posts and held it against the wire, plucking again. Instead of letting the sound ring out, he lifted the rock and placed it down. Hat Mang explained that holding the rock against the wire did something to the ‘pitch’. He plucked. Lift, place, pluck, repeat.


 ‘BWOW BWOW buh BWOW BWOW’ went the wire.”


Space Dad’s once-high voice suddenly deepened many octaves as he sang out the first song of Home.


“I died at that moment and began my life again. The surface of the River Tickle bubbled before my very eyes, the sky was lit up fluoro, and the air tasted of bee stings and the future. Blew my mind, you know?”   


‘What the heck is a bee?’ I thought, but didn’t interrupt.


“‘What the fuck was that?’ I spluttered. 
‘I’m gonna call it ‘music’, Hat Mang said.


 I had never heard anything like it, but I loved it immediately. It sounded like an old friend, a memory. 


I sat down. Hat Mang looked at me with the look of a shoplifter who’s just bagged a rotisserie snuffin from a chain supermarket. ‘But what it really needs is a beat’. 
‘What’s that?’ I asked. 
‘You know, when you beat stuff,’ said Hat Mang, handing me some sticks. 


That night, we introduced Home to music, and introduced the universe to the music of Home.” 


For a split second, my heart thumped in blast beat. I had always loved outsider art, and all those guys came from a world where music already existed. I couldn’t fathom what it would sound like when made by beings who had to invent it first. I needed to hear this.

Space Dad finished his Sprankle, now in a comfortable slump. He looked me in the eyes. 


“Hat Mang was always more interested in dreaming up instruments and recording devices. All of us found a role. The Dicken did the artwork for any release. The World’s Sneakiest War Criminal is my tour manager.  I’m into making songs. I’ve honed my craft and delighted my friends at Home. Now it’s time I share it with the rest of the universe.”


He fanned two tickets. CHAD WIFE, the print said—his stage name. [We share a stage name. A cosmic cowinkydink —Ed.]


“You should come along.” 


“You’re friends with a war criminal, I couldn’t support that” I said, a bit heart broken.
“It’s a name” laughed Space Dad. “Names aren’t facts.”

He handed me the tickets. His generosity took me. 


The same lime green as his shirt, they were on a strange fibrous stock. I read the text, matte-black in Helvetica Neue: 


“CHAD WIFE at the Docking Hub. Ghropsday, 4th of Mrunch, 28:00 p.m. Admit One”.


“What the heck is 28:00 p.m.?” I asked. 
“That’s Home time, baby!”

I smiled and closed my eyes, mulling over the rhythm in his words.
When I opened them, he was gone. In his seat was air. 

I looked down at the tickets in my hand. 
They were pristine.





Homecoming
Homecoming
I had three days. 
It usually took me weeks to prepare mentally for a proper outing, but I was curious what sort of crowd this guy would attract. Mostly, though, I just got lucky. 


BPM: 130
I left my house in Reensburn—a suburb in the town of Cahlmers, near the ghostly and abandoned Dock C. 


Reensburn was characterised by its towers made of wobbling shacks. They housed Earth non-eligibles of all ages, a few failing fishermangs.


Reensburn looked wary in the disappearing light of our shared Sun. The crumbling walls stood out from the artisinally textured exteriors of the inner sector. Don’t be fooled; while I was five when Reensburn got started, no Teauhan recalls a life before the inner sector.


I walked over to the Docking Hub in the chilly Cahlmers air, listening to the sub-filled groan of the floating foundations as they ground against the sea floor.      


Cahlmers didn’t feature in any Home & Habitat write-ups. With its soggy fishing docks and noisy nightlife, the so-called ‘capital of the Teauhan intergalactic fish trade’ attracted a certain type. 


Yet when I got there, I found myself staring. 


I clocked the queue. I recognised Snake-Legged Skaters from Gnar who swished on coiled tails, but the others were a mystery: 


Sentient mushrooms with beards as large as their busts; 
a biker jacket filled only with shimmering fog; 
a cherry tomato with two phallus-shaped legs, 


shouting “Watch your step!” in a deep booming baritone.


It harmonically clashed with the ever-present whine of drone propellers and sea-floor groan.


I watched with interest as the biker jacket started playing with the corners of a holographic CEN poster. 


The poster depicted a Happy Teauhan family eating cereal with bland corporate Teauhan art behind them, all sterile and cold. The text read: "Cultural Purity is Cultural Serenity. Report Unauthorised Art." 


The biker Jacket was suddenly focused as his fingers (hands?) seemed to dissipate under the adhesive. 


After a long minute, the biker jacket had done the job. The wall kept the shape of obedience for a breath; then let go with an eeeeer-
                                                               shlop. 


We all hated CEN—so I liked to think—but hating them in public took guts. He flicked his eyes upward, scanning for patrols. I walked up to himb and gave my extra ticket. He introduced himbself as Lyle. 


Part of me wanted to keep it, just in case no one else talked to me. If I gave it away, I’d be alone. But something about the kindness in his shimmer made me take the risk. 


Lyle was a bacterial hive mind from a galaxy the size of a small cow. “A passing space tanker spilt a bottle of hand sanitiser a bit too close,” he told me when I asked what prompted the move. 


Since then, he’d been living in a clump under Dock C—an abandoned part of town where the fogs were thick as soup—next to a nice warm sewage pipe. He apologised for the smell, but I told himb that I would be fine.

We settled in on the floor, about three rows from the front.

Space Dad’s hat rose above the crowd before I ever saw his face. It was then that I met Space Dad, the rock star. 


The first sound was wrong, and the last sound was the ocean remembering its childhood.


The arc was straightforward: start slow; accelerate.


Midway through the second track, something in my chest cracked open like an old fish crate. By the end, something else had moved in. Something warm and loud. 


After the show, as the crowd dispersed into the fog, Lyle pulled me aside. 


"That was incredible," he yelled. “I KNOW,” I yelled back. 


I was caked in sweat, fur matted, feeling good. 


We gushed and traded favourite moments. 


"And did you hear that feedback swell?" 
"Which one?" I asked, as there were many.
 "The one that lasted an age," Lyle laughed, "I swear an entire civilisation was birthed and fell in that squall."


When we reached the natural end of the conversation, he lowered his voice to a whisper. "But wasn't that third song, you know—" 
"Based on something from Earth?" I finished. "Yeah. I heard it too." 


"He's playing with fire," Lyle said. He wasn’t wrong. Drones were flying thick in the unusually fogless air that night. 


 "Maybe," I replied, "or maybe he's trying to warm the whole galaxy."  






The next morning, Space Dad found me crouched behind the bar at The Flotsam, drinking out of a chipped mug and scribbling in a notebook I didn’t know I owned. “You good?” he asked. 


“I may be having a moment,” I replied. 
“Cool,” he said. 


He smiled. It gave his bells hiccups. 


“I’m off today, but I wanted to thank you for befriending Lyle. He’s one of my best friends, and struggles to get people to treat himb well. I was interested in what you would do with the extra ticket. You’d fit in well on Home”. 


I couldn’t help myself, I grinned back. Wider than I had in years. Maybe ever. “You sure you’re not a Plantling?” he said, the smile adding a whistle to his standard bells and crackles. “We do have the best smiles, you know!”


He wrote a number down on the back of a coaster that said ‘Fish Till You’re Winning’. The disregard for my employer’s property tickled me.


“I don’t know when I’ll be back Home, but give me a buzz. We’d love to have you.” 


He came in close. Too close: “Remember, when confined, air beats nets.” He pulled away and walked towards the door, stopping at the threshold. 


“And Gharv? Share the warmth.”
Come Home
Come Home
In the years since I retired from adventuring, I’d be asked time and time again: “How did you get to Home?” Well, I still don’t know. But these are the events leading up to, and including, my first steps on that wonderful land. May it provide clues for those who need them.


BPM: 140
Space Dad always said I could visit when he got back to Home.


The problem was, he never really did. One show led to another. The Chaditional Wedding Tour spiralled into the Honeymoon Stretch, the Eternal Reception. 


The names changed, the venues got weirder, and Home kept getting pushed further into the future. 


After a blur of secondhand show reviews and static-laced bootlegs, I woke up one morning to discover five years had passed.


I didn’t get up to much. I worked at The Flotsam three days on, three days off. 


The rest of my time, I sat down at Dock C with Lyle amongst the junk.


Discarded baby monitors, tiny cries replaced by a low-batery hum. A broken radio with fog under the dials, which Lyle thumped whenever he walked past. A lil’ ritual I guess. It sent a small whiirrrr-ck-ck-ck out in response.

CEN patrols rarely came this way, and we were free to be ourselves.


We took turns picking books, and I would read them aloud in the light of day. Though I loved Lyle’s voice—which sounded like an aircon going through an autotuned harmony engine—he had to stay moist when the Sun was shining and our Teauhan books were still paper-based. 


When the Sun went over to visit Edenbrock (our sibling planet), we would talk about music, art, or just how we were feeling. Sitting under the light of an old, flickering billboard sending out a constant evolving bzzzzt whether noon or midnight: “Sprankle—Like An Orange Juice, But Greener”.


We never forgot about our dear friend Space Dad. 


We’d pull up the reviews for every show. Some shows went great, many were disasters. But by the end of the first year, everyone knew who he was. 


He played in the mine shafts of Klabzst, inspiring the miners to form the planet’s first union; the treetop villages of Ghambo where the amps sent psssts through the leaves, telling the wood secrets; the cheese bogs of Jarm where he changed nothing, and celebrated all he could. 


He even played at the palaces of Pilster—though he got banned for life after instructing the audience to “burn this hierarchical motherfucker to the ground.” Last I heard, Pilster is going through something of a cultural renaissance and seems to be doing quite well. 

We listened to the singles as soon as they dropped. We even got copies of deleted uploads from a Russian Earthling hacker who wouldn’t disclose his current planetary location, fanmanging over the way Space Dad constantly seemed to be improving. 


We almost died when a tinny cellphone recording in Bromfenburst captured himb introducing a particularly shambolic cover of Gold Soundz with a casual “This one goes out to Gharv and Lyle. It might be odd to dedicate a breakup song to two best friends, but to me, this song has always sounded like friendship.”

To us it did too.


We catalogued his songs and shared his stories, but an unsettling buzz persisted. I wasn’t settled, the VU needle on zero when the amps were turned up full. 


Parts in place, no signal.                                             A broken lead.

I waited. I itched. I tried not to think too hard about it. I kept that coaster tucked inside the back cover of my notebook, just in case. I was happy with Lyle and The Flotsam, but I wanted the chance to reconnect with that tall orange mang with the bells in his chest. 


After three years, the edges were soft as old cloth. After four, the number was a blue smudge. I’d trace it with my finger, trying to remember the exact shape of the ink, trying to convince myself it was a promise, not a memory. Lyle would watch me, his shimmer a little dimmer on those days.


My fur started to permanently stand on end, as if even my hair follicles wanted to blast out of Teauh. I even stopped trying at the Flotsam. 


Now nobody cleaned the glasses. Eahrl got violently sick off old fish guts residue and hasn’t been seen for a couple beats.

In an attempt to get rid of the itch, I took to pacing—a habit I thought I had lost in my teenage years. 


One day, Lyle spoke up. “You’re going to wear a hole in the dock if you keep going,” he said, in a bit of a mood. “You need to do something.”
“Like what?” I retorted. “I don’t know, mang. It’s like Space Dad says—you need to chill out, go with the flow.”


But what does that even mean? Was I just meant to rot in a corner with a broken radio and call it Mlembian meditation?


I stopped in my tracks. “What if I just… called?” 
“Then I think,” he said, lightening up, “you’d better pack.”


The coaster number rang four times, the bell of The Flotsam’s old phone clanging against the walls and bouncing around my skull, before a voice spiralled through the electronics, ‘Dock C. Shlorpsday. 17:00 a.m. Dress slutty.’ I stared at the phone, a grin splitting my face in half.


The hard tone of hanging up pierced through the earpiece… The crash cymbal of the “tt” and the bubbling of a freshwater spring from the “sl” seemed to ring through the air.

[1] The best coffee is Special Blend granulated ($2.30 from your supermarket). The tendency is to make it too strong but a lightly-heaped teaspoon is plenty; any more takes away from the special blend experience. This is the dosage at which Special Blend shines. In terms of flavour topology, most coffee is a wide semi-circle shape: the flavour hits about five millimeters down the tongue then spreads out. The semi-circles’s edge tastes unpleasant, no matter how smooth & rich the center-flavour is. A ‘real’ coffee lines the tongue with a sour tang which instantly distracts, enough to make one reflexively flatten the organ to escape the flavour- big mistake. Past the tongue, in the cheek, that’s where coffee’s worst flavour offender hangs out: a bitter astringency that tastes like oven cleaner


When made properly, the flavour of Special Blend hits your tongue in a straight line, like pig slop down a feeding slide. Pure coffee flavour, refined.The taste rolls from cup down esophagus with the grace of Audrey Hepburn. Espresso clings, trying to prove its elegance. Special blend is confident and doesn't outstay its welcome. There’s just something special about it. 



[2] I didn’t laugh at all. I know what this is like. It started with meditation. I started at one minute a day. It was all I could manage, and it felt excruciating. However, for some unknown reason, I kept returning to it, day after day. Maybe for the same reason, I vacuumed my room daily—I just wanted to do something that felt like a good thing to do. 
The next four months were bliss. I was energetic and helped my dad build a wall over a series of weekends. I felt valuable and good. I picked up the guitar and started writing songs. My meditation practice had extended to three hours a day. What began as simply clearing my mind had become a process of experiencing sensations “at the level of the actual sensations that make up experiences” (Daniel Ingram). What had started off as feeling large patches on the surface of my body turned into locating smaller and smaller sensations. The meditation session that changed everything started as all the others. I had just reached six-hour sessions two days before. Like those two days (and many days before), I sat down on my DIY meditation bench made from slats taken from an old single bed and went through some recognizable stages.


As I progressed through the stages of meditation, I began noticing sensations as discrete points, each flickering in and out of existence almost as quickly as it arrived. It became clear that I could only feel one sensation at a time . What had previously seemed like solid, continuous perceptions revealed themselves as collections of smaller, momentary blips, each arising and vanishing independently. I didn’t realize it at the time, but I was already moving away from static forms and toward something modular—something more akin to how a machine processes information in discrete, reusable flows.
As I deepened my practice experiences become subtle, diffuse, and almost insubstantial​. I started noticing the space between sensations—tiny gaps of nothingness that seemed to outnumber the sensations themselves. The more I paid attention, the more these gaps expanded. Everything I had previously known as experience seemed to be unraveling.
Then, suddenly, without warning, the entire field of my experience ruptured. I was no longer in my body. I had rushed out of it altogether. Looking down, I saw my meditating form below, but it was no longer solid—it was a constellation of pulsating blue sensation points. 
Before I could grasp what was happening, my blue constellation self was drawn into a space in my right knee. Inside, I experienced a whole new universe. There was no separation between ‘I’ and what I perceived—everything simply occurred in a seamless field. Strangely, despite my entire perception of self-shifting, my right knee on my pulsating form remained intact, I just also existed within it.
When I opened my eyes, reality had not returned as expected. I felt wrong. I wasn’t refreshed, I wasn’t tired - the normal two options after a successful or unsuccessful attempt at meditation. I was nothing. I went downstairs and made some toast, but when I ate it, I couldn’t tell if I liked the taste. The usual reference points for preference were gone. The sense of a stable, coherent ‘me' had been completely dislodged. This time, the loss of the I was sudden and immediate. I didn’t as much ‘lose myself’ as had the self ripped from my body.
Then came the paralysis.
I would sit down to play guitar, my fingers poised on the strings, waiting for a note, any note. But nothing came. Not because I was out of ideas but because the concept of making a choice felt foreign. How could I choose a note if I wasn’t sure there was a "me" to choose it? There was no ‘me’ to have ideas. Thoughts happened, I’m sure they did, but I lacked the constructs to make meaning out of them. Time would slip away. With the guitar in my hands, I’d star at the wall; the next thing I knew the entire day had passed. This happened repeatedly, as if I were dissolving into the background of my own life.
Somewhere in the back of my mind, I recognized that something had gone terribly wrong. But even that recognition felt detached, like watching a character in a film realize they're lost but being unable to feel concern for them. I had sought to explore the nature of consciousness and ended up erasing the mechanisms that made it feel coherent.
The days blurred together. At first, I thought it was just an adjustment phase, that I had reached some new stage of perception and would soon find my footing. Then weeks passed. Then months. I began to feel a creeping panic, but even panic felt strangely impersonal, like it was happening to someone else and I was just a distant observer. I wasn’t depressed in the traditional sense—there was no sadness, no longing, no inner torment. But there was something worse: absence. The total erosion of anything that made life feel like it belonged to me. A pit.
The “I” is not real. But without it neither are we.
A paradox. 
Something impossible to solve. —Ed.
